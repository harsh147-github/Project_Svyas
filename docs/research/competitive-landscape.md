# Competitive landscape

## Existing government systems

### CPGRAMS (Centralized Public Grievance Redress and Monitoring System)

**Scale:**
- 11.2 million grievances handled between 2019-2024
- Currently processing 2.6 million grievances annually
- 95% resolution rate
- Connected to all central and state government ministries
- Mandatory system for government officials to monitor

**Current limitations (per World Bank analysis):**
- Complex forms and rigid categories
- Text-heavy interfaces requiring digital skills
- Administrative processes prioritized over user-friendliness
- Not accessible in informal language

### NextGen CPGRAMS - AI modernization in progress

**Status:** Tender issued October 2024, closed January 2025. Likely in implementation now.

**Planned features (from government tender documents):**
- Natural Language Processing to understand informal multilingual complaints
- Example given: "paani nahi aa raha" automatically routes to water department
- AI synthesis of grievance patterns
- Automatic classification and routing to relevant authorities
- Enhanced transparency dashboards
- Integration with existing 17 fragmented government systems
- Predictive analytics to forecast grievance spikes

**Partners involved:**
- World Bank's CIVIC AI Collaborative
- IIT Kanpur (already built IGMS 2.0 dashboard)
- Department of Administrative Reforms & Public Grievances (DARPG)
- Multiple AI vendors competing for implementation contract

**This is nearly identical to what Sushaasan proposes to build.**

## State and local systems

### PuraSeva (Andhra Pradesh)
- Fully integrated citizen and employee mobile app
- Real-time dashboards for officials
- SLA tracking with automatic escalation
- Direct connection between citizens and municipal employees
- 70% of users report improved perception of local government

### Civinc (Nanjangud, Karnataka)
- Ward-specific QR codes connecting citizens to responsible officials
- Zero-cost model for municipalities
- Built by students from NIT Jamshedpur and Ashoka University
- Scaling to other non-metro cities

## Why this matters

**Government buy-in challenge:**
Officials are mandated to use CPGRAMS. Any competing platform faces:
- Regulatory requirement to use the official system
- No incentive to adopt a parallel unofficial platform
- Risk of fragmentation if grievances are split across systems
- Lack of authority for a private platform to assign work to government officials

**Citizen adoption challenge:**
Even if Sushaasan offers a better experience:
- CPGRAMS is being rebuilt with the same AI features (multilingual NLP, synthesis, routing)
- Citizens already know to use government portals for official redressal
- A forum discussion platform (like Reddit) serves a different psychological purpose than filing an official complaint
- Unclear why someone would use Sushaasan instead of the official system once NextGen CPGRAMS launches

## What would differentiate Sushaasan?

This needs validation with actual users, but possible angles:
- **Pre-decision input**: Help government gather public input *before* making decisions (not just handling complaints after problems arise)
- **Community discussion**: Focus on the forum aspect where citizens help each other and build collective understanding
- **Non-governmental problems**: Civic issues that don't require official action but benefit from community coordination
- **Synthesis for policy**: Different from grievance tracking - synthesizing public opinion for policy decisions

All of these would require testing whether citizens and officials actually want them.
