## Questions for civic tech builders (IIT Kanpur, CPGRAMS team, Civinc)

### Understanding current limitations

1. **Adoption gap:** CPGRAMS handles 2.6M grievances but that's only 0.19% of Indians. What's preventing wider adoption? Is it UX, awareness, trust, or something else?
2. **Resolution vs action:** 95% resolution rate sounds good, but what does "resolved" actually mean? How satisfied are citizens with outcomes?
3. **NextGen CPGRAMS plans:** What AI features are being built? Multilingual NLP, synthesis, routing — what else? When will it launch?
4. **What you learned:** What surprised you most while building/studying these systems? What assumptions turned out to be wrong?

### Testing the "collective discussion" angle

1. **Individual vs collective:** Current systems track individual grievances. Have you explored collective discussion synthesis? If yes, what did you find? If no, why not?
2. **Anonymous input:** CPGRAMS requires identity verification. What's the trade-off between accountability and getting honest input? Would officials trust anonymous discussions?
3. **Pre-decision input:** Can you share examples where government gathered public input BEFORE making decisions (not just complaints after)? How did that work?

### Technical feasibility

1. **Synthesis quality:** For AI synthesis of informal discussions — what's actually possible vs what's theoretical? Can you point to working examples?
2. **"Optimized solutions":** The idea of a neural network generating optimized solutions from civic discussion — is this technically feasible or science fiction? What would it take?
3. **Real-time processing:** Processing millions of comments in real-time across multiple languages — what's the infrastructure requirement? What does that cost?

### What government actually needs

1. **Official pain points:** What do municipal/state officials actually struggle with? Is it lack of information, lack of budget, lack of authority, political constraints?
2. **Dashboard adoption:** Officials are mandated to use CPGRAMS. Would they monitor an additional unofficial platform? Under what conditions?
3. **Trust in synthesis:** Would officials act on AI-synthesized outputs from anonymous forum discussions? What would make them trust it?

## Questions for government officials (municipal level)

### Current state

1. **Your process now:** How do you currently learn about civic issues in your area? CPGRAMS, direct complaints, media, social media, site visits?
2. **Volume and capacity:** How many complaints/grievances do you handle? What percentage can you actually address given budget and authority constraints?
3. **CPGRAMS experience:** What works well about CPGRAMS? What's frustrating? Do you find it useful?

### Testing Sushaasan value proposition

1. **Collective intelligence:** Would it be valuable to see "40,000 people discussed water issues in your ward, here's the AI synthesis of root causes and suggested solutions" instead of individual complaints?
2. **Anonymous discussions:** Would you trust synthesis from anonymous forum discussions, or do you need verified identities?
3. **Pre-decision input:** Have you ever wanted to gather public opinion BEFORE making a decision? How did you do it? What would make that easier?
4. **Social media monitoring:** Do you monitor Twitter/WhatsApp/Reddit for civic issues in your area? Why or why not?

### Practical adoption

1. **Another platform?** If there was a system better than CPGRAMS, would you use it? Or are you locked into official systems?
2. **What would make you adopt:** What would a new platform need to offer for you to actually monitor and use it regularly?
3. **Transparency concerns:** Would you be comfortable with synthesized solutions being publicly visible before you can act on them? Any concerns about that?

## What would change your direction

After these conversations, you should be able to answer:

**Green lights (keep going):**

- Officials confirm they would actually monitor and act on collective synthesis
- Clear gap identified between what CPGRAMS offers and what's needed
- Technical feasibility confirmed by teams already building this
- Path to adoption is clearer than "build it and hope they come"

**Yellow lights (pivot approach):**

- Officials interested BUT need different format/integration
- Collective synthesis is valuable BUT needs to be inside CPGRAMS not separate
- Citizens would use it BUT distribution strategy needs rethinking

**Red lights (fundamental problem):**

- Officials won't use unofficial platforms regardless of quality
- NextGen CPGRAMS already addresses everything you're proposing
- Technical feasibility of "optimized solution generation" is not realistic
- No viable path to simultaneous two-sided adoption

## How to approach these conversations

**Frame it as learning, not pitching:**

- "I'm exploring whether collective discussion synthesis could complement CPGRAMS"
- "I want to understand what actually prevents wider civic participation"
- "I'm trying to learn from people who've built in this space"

**Be honest about your stage:**

- You have a concept and landing page
- You're trying to validate before building
- You want to learn whether this is worth pursuing

**Listen for what they DON'T say:**

- If they're polite but don't engage deeply, that's a signal
- If they say "interesting idea" but don't ask follow-ups, that's a signal
- If they redirect you to existing solutions, that's a signal

**Ask for introductions:**

- "Who else should I talk to about this?"
- "Are there people working on related problems I should learn from?"
