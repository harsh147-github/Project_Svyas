The broken system

Parliament decides what to discuss by lottery. Not by what matters to you.

In Question Hour — the one hour meant to hold the government accountable — which topics get raised is decided by ballot. A lottery. Not by urgency. Not by how many citizens are affected. A random draw decides whether your water crisis gets 60 seconds of attention or gets ignored entirely.

And even that one hour is routinely destroyed. The 17th Lok Sabha lost 387 hours to disruptions — walkouts, sloganeering, adjournments. The opposition roasts the government. The government roasts the opposition. The public watches from home and wonders: when will someone discuss MY road, MY water, MY child’s school?

Parliament met for just 55 days per year in the 17th Lok Sabha — down from 135 days in the 1950s. The UK Parliament sits 150+ days. The US Congress sits 260 days. India, the world’s largest democracy, sits the least.

42% of bills were passed with less than 30 minutes of debate. The Farm Laws — affecting 600 million farmers — were passed in 7 minutes in Rajya Sabha. Seven minutes. No proper vote. No proper discussion. Your livelihood, decided faster than a pizza delivery.

The failed solutions

Every government app built for citizens has failed. Because they were built for government, not people.

The government keeps building apps that nobody wants to use. Not because people don’t care — because the apps are complicated, require 15 form fields, ask for Aadhaar numbers, crash on basic phones, and mark complaints as “resolved” without doing anything. Citizens don’t have time for this. They’re working, commuting, surviving. If contributing to democracy requires filling a 10-field form, democracy loses.

PMC Road Mitra

Launched Aug 2025 to report potholes. Died within 6 months. Officials didn’t even know it stopped working.

PMC Care App

Complaints marked “resolved” without action. Can’t load Google Maps. Submit button breaks. 2023 tickets buried under 2025 ones.

Every other grievance portal

Complex forms. No follow-up. No synthesis. No transparency. One complaint in, zero change out. Repeat forever.

What if contributing took 10 seconds instead of 10 minutes? What if you just texted what you see — like telling a friend — and the system did the rest?

Every day, 1.4 billion Indians complain about the same things — roads, water, traffic, corruption, slow government. We post on Twitter. We rant on WhatsApp. We blame politicians. We blame each other. And nothing changes. What if we took all that energy and pointed it in the same direction? Not as a protest. Not as a movement. Just as a nation that finally decided to work as a team — each person contributing one small piece of what they know, AI assembling it into something no authority can ignore.

The solution

SUAS: the real-time nervous system between citizens and action.

Not a complaint box. Not a petition tool. Not another app that dies in six months. A continuous, living expression of what the community knows, wants, and has figured out — structured by AI into something any decision-maker can act on immediately.

The autorickshaw driver knows which junction needs a signal. The chai wallah knows which drain overflows every monsoon. Every opinion is a puzzle piece. SUAS assembles the puzzle.

Voting says “I choose you.” SUAS says “here’s what we need you to do, here’s how, and 843 of us are watching.”

10 seconds. That’s all it takes.

The model

Citizens are contributors. They submit lived experience and solutions. AI synthesizes it into a pull request — structured, optimized, with confidence scores. The decision-maker is the reviewer — secretary, corporator, collector, minister. They accept, request changes, or ignore — but ignoring is public, timestamped, and everyone sees it.
